The Importance of Sabr

We often hear the phrase, “Patience is a part of faith.” This originates from the words of ‘Abdullah ibn Mas’ud (RA) as recorded in Shu’ab al-Iman 9083, and authenticated by Ibn Hajar al-‘Asqalani (RH). However, the phrase “a part” can be misleading, as in English, it may imply an undefined portion rather than an equal division. The more accurate translation is “half.” The full hadith is as follows:

الصَّبْرُ نِصْفُ الإِيمَانِ وَالْيَقِينُ الإِيمَانُ كُلُّهُ

“PATIENCE is half of faith, and CERTAINTY is faith in its entirety.”

And this statement originates from the Verses of the Quran, where patience is paired with the other half of faith—gratitude. For instance, in Verse 31 of Surah Luqman, Allah (SWT) says:

أَلَمۡ تَرَ أَنَّ ٱلۡفُلۡكَ تَجۡرِي فِي ٱلۡبَحۡرِ بِنِعۡمَتِ ٱللَّهِ لِيُرِيَكُم مِّنۡ ءَايَٰتِهِۦٓۚ إِنَّ فِي ذَٰلِكَ لَأٓيَٰتࣲ لِّكُلِّ صَبَّارࣲ شَكُورࣲ

Do you not see that the ships sail upon the sea by the grace of Allah, so that He may show you some of His signs? Indeed, in this are signs for those who are PATIENT and GRATEFUL.

Then, there is also a hadith of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in Sahih Muslim 35, which states that faith consists of more than seventy branches. How do we understand this? To make it clearer, let us use an analogy.

Imagine faith as a planet within our hearts. At its very core lies certainty—without absolute certainty, the planet of faith cannot exist. Surrounding this core is the mantle, representing patience and gratitude, which form the foundation that upholds faith. Finally, on the crust or surface, the seventy-plus branches of faith are spread out, manifesting in various aspects of belief and practice.

From this, we begin to understand the immense importance of patience in living a life of faith. Without patience, the perfection of faith cannot be attained. In fact, patience is a virtue placed at an exceptionally high rank, as Allah (AWJ) declares in Verse 10 of Surah Az-Zumar:

إِنَّمَا يُوَفَّى ٱلصَّٰبِرُونَ أَجۡرَهُم بِغَيۡرِ حِسَابࣲ

Only those who are patient will be given their reward without limit.

Not only that, but without patience, the core of certainty will begin to crack, the mantle of gratitude will weaken, and the branches of faith on the surface will erode. Without patience, the planet of faith will shatter. This aligns with the words of ‘Ali (RA) in As-Sabr by Ibn Abi Dunya 8:

أَلَا إِنَّ الصَّبْرَ مِنَ الْإِيمَانِ بِمَنْزِلَةِ الرَّأْسِ مِنَ الْجَسَدِ فَإِذَا قُطِعَ الرَّأْسُ بَادَ الْجَسَدُ أَلَا إِنَّهُ لَا إِيمَانَ لِمَنْ لَا صَبْرَ لَهُ

“Surely, patience in relation to faith is like the head to the body—if the head is severed, the body perishes. Indeed, there is no faith for the one who has no patience.”

The Imagery of Sabr

Before delving into the hadith about patience being like grasping hot embers, we must first ensure we truly understand the meaning of “sabr.” A direct translation into English alone is insufficient, as modern languages lack the depth of the most precise language in the world. As explained in Verse 2 of Surah Yusuf, this is why the Quran was revealed in Classical Arabic—so that we may grasp its terms profoundly and apply them correctly in our lives.

To fully grasp “sabr,” we must explore its linguistic depth in Arabic. Unlike most languages, where words are formed by combining letters and modified with prefixes and suffixes, Classical Arabic is built upon triliteral roots—three base letters that generate numerous interconnected words, each carrying its own meaning.

For instance, the word صَبْر, derives from the root ص ب ر. Each letter holds significance: where ص signifies “consistency” or “homogeneity,” ب represents “medium” or “conveyance,” and ر implies “command” or “order.” Together, they convey the idea of “consistency that brings order”—the very essence of patience. Such depth of meaning is unparalleled in any other language.

Arabic is a language of clarity, deeply rooted in real, observable scenarios. Unlike many other languages that rely on abstract terms, Arabic words capture vivid, tangible meanings. A single word can encapsulate entire descriptions, unfolding layers of imagery that other languages struggle to convey.

The word صَبْر is a clear example. Among the words derived from its triliteral root is صَابُورَة—a ballast system in which heavy stones were placed beneath the deck of ancient Arab sailing vessels to maintain balance amid towering waves. This reflects the nature of patience: not only a force that stabilises faith, but one that prevents a person from being swept away by the turbulence of life.

Another word originating from the same triliteral root is صَبِرٌ, which refers to the aloe plant, the extracted juice of certain bitter trees, and potent medicinal substances. Though intensely bitter, these were highly valued for their healing properties. From this, we understand its connection to the nature of “sabr”—a bitter experience to endure, yet profoundly beneficial; difficult to bear, yet essential for physical, emotional, and spiritual resilience.

Moreover, صَبْرَةٌ, which signifies strong stones or iron, reflects the resilience that emerges from the practice of patience. Just as stone and iron withstand external pressure without breaking, “sabr” fortifies an individual against life's trials. Through each hardship endured, a person grows stronger, much like metal tempered by fire. The more challenges one faces, the more one’s wisdom deepens, and one’s perseverance strengthens in confronting the trials ahead.

In the daily lives of the ancient Arabs, farmers would amass vast quantities of grain—such as wheat—during harvest. The sheer volume was so immense that when heaped together, it remained unmeasured and unweighed, forming a towering heap known as صُبْرَةٌ. This term embodies vastness and immensity—analogous to the limitless reward of “sabr,” as mentioned earlier in Verse 10 of Surah Az-Zumar. Indeed, the hardships endured with patience are profoundly worthwhile.

To conclude this section, livestock such as sheep, goats, and camels that instinctively return to their owners by dusk, following the same familiar path, are referred to as أَصْبِرَةٌ. This reflects the obedience inherent in the nature of “sabr.” Just as these animals return without hesitation to their owners, the devoted servants of Allah remain steadfast upon the straight path set by their Creator, always turning back to His guidance. Just as an owner cherishes the animals that remain loyal to them, Allah loves those who patiently persevere, as conveyed in Verse 146 of Surah aal-e-‘Imran.

وَٱللَّهُ يُحِبُّ ٱلصَّٰبِرِينَ

And Allah loves the patient ones.

The Seven Meanings of Sabr

Arabic is a language rich with layered meanings. A single word can branch out into multiple interpretations, each carrying its own tone, character, and function—yet all remain interconnected. To truly understand the concept of “sabr” in modern languages, we must first identify the seven meanings that emerge from its root. To do so, we will explore how each of these meanings is used in the Verses of the Quran.

The first meaning is PATIENCE, which refers to the ability to accept or tolerate difficult situations without becoming annoyed or anxious. This quality is essential when facing trials such as boredom, delays, disruptions, irritation, hardship, pain, suffering, and the like.

It requires a person to calm the heart, as expressed in the saying of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ recorded in Musnad Ahmad 23122: “Whoever succumbs to anxiety will suffer the harm that anxiety brings.” It also calls for a sense of contentment with Divine decree, as demonstrated in the words of Prophet Jacob (AS) in Verse 18 of Surah Yusuf: “So I shall bear this with beautiful PATIENCE”—that is, a patience marked by inner calm and free from complaint, protest, or resentment.
