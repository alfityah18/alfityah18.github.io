The Origin of “Khutaba”

In the context of ancient Arabia, a khatib assumed the mantle of a prominent tribal spokesperson during the era of Jahiliyyah and the early stages of Islam. His role was to impart exhortations and admonishments, known as khutbah, from the pulpit; which encompassed various forms of expression, including sermon, speech, oration, or discourse, often recited in rhyming prose, and also mixed with verses of poetry. The "khutbah’s" roots are believed to trace back to the utterances of the khatib, who skillfully employed beautiful prose to celebrate the virtues and the achievements of their tribe members while disparaging the weaknesses and the losses of their adversaries. This tradition offered a very effective platform for conveying important messages and also rallying tribal sentiment.

The arrival of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ heralded a transformative elevation and revolutionization of the old khutbah. This evolution entailed a shift from a role that once fueled tribal animosity through preaching to that of a leader who actively promotes unity under one religion. Also, the transition extended from the communication of tribe-specific messages to the diffusion of universally beneficial knowledge. Further transformation was seen in the approach, as it evolved from relying on eloquent prose based on personal opinions and human ideas to embracing distinct guidance rooted in the teachings of the Quran and Sunnah. These radical shifts, embraced and exemplified by the Companions of the Prophet ﷺ, have shaped the khutbah into what we recognize today.

2. Intention

Captivated by the fame and riches of one celebrity preacher, X was inspired to delve into religious studies. This led him to pursue education at an Islamic university in the Arabian Peninsula. After obtaining his degree, by emulating his icon, he turned into a celebrity preacher himself. He began to hold his knowledge in high regard, deeming himself more intelligent, insightful, and even more educated than others. He harnessed his acquired knowledge to contend with the ulama, debate those less informed, and captivate attention by demonstrating his mastery in memorizing the Quran, Hadith, and scholarly works. After gaining fame and wealth, he ceased to seek further knowledge, convinced that he possessed enough. His followers hailed him as a renowned scholar. And so, he lives contentedly, savoring all the respect and praise.

In this fictional story, which part of X’s journey do you believe he did wrong? The most obvious is when he became arrogant about his acquired knowledge. As stated in Musnad Abi Ya’la 6698, Rasulullah ﷺ said that: “They are among you from this ummah, and they are the fuel for the Hellfire.” But not everyone knows that apart from pursuing beneficial knowledge, every aspect of X appears to be misguided, right from the start. The love of fame marks the inception of all evil. X embarked on his journey with fame and wealth, clearly displaying worldly intentions, a path that Prophet Muhammad ﷺ cautioned against in Sunan Abi Dawud 3664: “Whoever acquires knowledge that should be sought seeking the pleasure of Allah (AWJ), but acquires it only to gain some worldly advantage, will not experience the fragrance of Paradise.” X should have known this better.

Even though he already possessed knowledge, X made no effort to correct his ill intentions; in fact, they grew worse. His routine of criticizing scholars, arguing with the ignorant, and seeking the limelight served as reasons for Allah (SWT) to admit him into the Hellfire, as conveyed in the words of Rasulullah ﷺ in Jami’ at-Tirmidhi 2654. Despite being hailed as a scholar by the public, his obsession with his own knowledge revealed that he was, in fact, a jahil. This sentiment echoes the words of Masruq ibn al-Ajda’ (RH) in Sunan ad-Darimi 385. And his decision to cease seeking knowledge does not align with the qualities of a true scholar. For in Jami’ at-Tirmidhi 2686, the Prophet ﷺ stated: “The believer will never be satisfied with goodness he hears, until he ends up in Paradise.” The ending of X's story may seem like a good ending, but there is a twist. As this culture has been rooted since its re-emergence, subsequent generations no longer regard it as a threat. If many religious figures start to pursue fame and wealth, next generation will regard it as an honorable pursuit. This is contrary! 3. Knowledge

Imagine two clear bottles placed side by side. From a distance, they appear identical, each containing a clear, transparent liquid. However, only upon closer inspection—through smell or taste—can you discern their true nature. One bottle holds pure water, quenching thirst and sustaining life. The other bottle contains rubbing alcohol, which, despite its pristine appearance, projects a pungent odour and is harmful if ingested. Such is the analogy of the marfu’ hadith above. Broadly speaking, most of the bearers of religious knowledge either appear to base their knowledge on the Quran and Hadith or explicitly claim to do so. In reality, they are divided into two groups: only one is authentic, while the others deceive and bring harm; only one is true scholars, while the others are akin to the preachers of the age of Jahiliyyah.

Beyond depth of knowledge, another aspect that distinguishes true scholars from impostors is how they approach religious knowledge. Genuine scholars are fair, honest, upright, and faithful to the Quran and Hadith: they preserve these texts as they were revealed, inherited, and practised by the earliest generations, even safeguarding them from any form of corruption. Unlike worldly knowledge, which undergoes evolution, divine knowledge is already perfect, as stated by Allah (AWJ) in Verse 3 of Surah al-Maidah. Thus, something that has already attained a state of perfection risks disruption of its equilibrium and potential destruction if manipulated. As mentioned in Sahih Muslim 1920, they will always be triumphant on the path of truth, and they are akin to the bottle that holds pure water required by this ummah.

On the path of falsehood, there exist three groups of the Jahiliyyah-era preachers, regardless of their titles, certificates, influence, or even position. The first group comprises the ghuluw who tarnish the moderation of the way of life established by Allah. They result in ruin not only to matters of ibadah but also to aqidah, potentially leading to the collapse of Tawhid, as indicated in Verse 77 of Surah al-Maidah. The second group consists of liars who treat religious knowledge as if it originates from them, prioritizing intellect, interpreting the Quran according to their desires, fabricating Hadith, using dreams and the words of figures that contradict the words of Allah and the sayings of His Messenger as arguments, and even inventing practices. And the third group is made up of the unintelligent and those with darkened hearts—camel calves being milked, lacking understanding, and failing to apply their knowledge—disseminating inaccuracies.

However, differentiating those two bottles is not as straightforward as one might think. Through different texts in Silsilah as-Sahihah 3189 below, to distinguish knowledge in an era where true scholars are scarce and preachers are abundant, Rasulullah ﷺ explained that the sensor one must possess is the divine knowledge that enlightens the heart. Nothing can evaluate knowledge except knowledge itself. This is crucial at a time when knowledge is becoming corrupted, and people start to regard preachers as scholars. Moreover, to be protected by Allah, knowledge must be pursued with ikhlas, as stated in Verse 40 of Surah al-Hijr. Those who practice knowledge can taste the difference between the bottles. Those who seek knowledge can smell their distinction. Those who disregard knowledge are prone to deception.

5. Aqidah

In Sahih al-Bukhari 7084, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ explained that following an era tainted with wickedness, during which deviant sects would emerge, there would come a time when some preachers would invite people to Jahannam. This signifies that after the emergence of the seventy-two misguided sects, as mentioned by the Prophet ﷺ in Sunan Ibn Majah 3992, an era would arise where the doctrines of these sects—originating from the six primary groups: Rafidhah, Haruriyyah, Jahmiyyah, Murjiah, Qadariyyah, and Jabriyyah—would be spread publicly and extensively. We are arguably in that phase now, perhaps even at its peak. Today, on social media, without any theological censorship, anyone can become a preacher and disseminate any form of teaching. Hence, the ummah is profoundly exposed to significant danger.

Moreover, it is even more concerning when we learn from Musnad ash-Shamiyyin 588 that these sects are not only capable of overstepping boundaries, lying, and misinterpreting the Quran and Hadith, but they can also do the same to the established madhhabs of Ahlu Sunnah by pretending to be part of one of the madhhabs. When their corrupt teachings have long been profoundly embedded in the minds of the people, efforts at remedy will often be rejected and resisted, since their deceits are perceived as the Sunnah of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. This is the sad reality mentioned by Rasulullah ﷺ in Sahih at-Targhib 111. That is why, as mentioned in Al-Muwatta’ 1661, he reminded his followers to hold firmly to the Quran and Sunnah, so they can separate the wheat from the chaff, discerning between the genuine and the false.

It should be noted that preachers who lead people to Hellfire can be divided into two types: those who call to a temporary punishment and those who call to an eternal one. No corruption of aqidah is more frightening than the destruction of Tawhid. The same problem that plagued previous ummahs and some segment of this ummah is the extreme adoration for pious individuals. In Sahih al-Bukhari 3873, Rasulullah ﷺ mentioned that they will be resurrected as the worst of creatures on the Day of Resurrection. Their modus operandi involves worshipping at the graves of the pious, praying to them, and seeking help from their souls, which they claim are close to Allah (SWT). This ignorance has been ongoing since the time of Prophet Noah (AS), as detailed in Verse 23 of Surah Nuh, and it is the primary subterfuge and mission of Iblis.

Regardless of whether a person is born a Muslim, or their good deeds are as high as the mountains, if one dies committing Major Shirk such as those, all their deeds will be shown before their eyes and disappear like dust in the wind, as described in Verse 23 of Surah al-Furqan. Subsequently, they will be cast into Jahannam to suffer eternally, for, as stated by Allah in Verse 48 of Surah an-Nisa, He will never forgive the sin of associating partners with Him. So, how can such preachers exist? In addition to Verse 2-3 of Surah al-Insan cited earlier, the answer is also present in Verse 76 of Surah az-Zukhruf: it is their own choice. The detachment of society from understanding the contents of the Quran and listening to the Sunnah, not recognising the true aqidah of the four major madhhabs, and treating religion merely as a tradition or imitation are among the reasons some are deceived.
